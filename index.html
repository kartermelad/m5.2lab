The Trouble with Bears

By Evan Wild

Tall, lumbering, angry, and dangerous — real live bears are proud, independent creatures, always on the hunt for food. They’re nothing like the bears you see in fictional portrayals, such as Baloo from The Jungle Book.

So what are bears really like, and why does the world's media portray them with such a skewed vision? In this article, we’ll try to answer those questions and give you real insight into the life of a bear.

Types of Bear

Bears generally come in two varieties — large and medium. Small bears don’t truly exist; if you see a smaller bear, it’s more than likely a bear cub of a larger species. Bears can also be classified by habitat: both large and medium bears can thrive in urban or countryside environments.

The following table provides some useful facts about wild and urban bears:

Comparison of Wild and Urban Bears
Bear Type Coat Adult size Habitat Lifespan Diet
Wild Brown or black 1.4 to 2.8 meters Woods and forests 25 to 28 years Fish, meat, plants
Urban North Face 18 to 22 Condos and coffee shops 20 to 32 years Starbucks, sushi

Habitats and Eating Habits

Wild bears eat a varied diet of meat, fish, fruit, nuts, and other naturally growing ingredients. They typically hunt for food in woodlands and rivers, but may seek sustenance from farms or country houses. They often live in caves, tents, or cottages away from crowded populations.

Urban (gentrified) bears have largely adopted different living patterns, frequenting dumpsters, bins, or fast-food restaurants. They’re known to sleep in bus shelters, parks, or even their own apartments. They may occasionally hunt urban wildlife, but typically scavenge from readily available sources.

Mating Rituals

Bears are romantic creatures by nature, generally seeking a lifelong mate. They decorate their dwellings to impress potential partners: wild bears may use cave paintings or beds of reeds, while urban bears opt for mood lighting and a classic Michael Bublé soundtrack.
